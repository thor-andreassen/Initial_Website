About Me

Dr. Thor E. Andreassen is a postdoctoral research fellow at Mayo Clinic, funded by a National Institute of Arthritis and Musculoskeletal and Skin Diseases (NIAMS) T32 grant under the mentorship of Dr. Kristin D. Zhao. With a PhD and MS in Mechanical Engineering from the University of Denver and a BS in Mechanical Engineering from the Colorado School of Mines, Dr. Andreassen brings extensive expertise in biomechanical modeling and simulation.

His research focuses on developing computational models of the musculoskeletal system to better understand joint mechanics and improve clinical outcomes.

Open Science Contributions

Dr. Andreassen is committed to open science and reproducible research. He has provided significant contributions to the scientific community by making his research models, data, and software openly available:

  • Open-source computational models for musculoskeletal systems
  • Publicly accessible datasets from in vivo biomechanical studies
  • Software tools for finite element analysis and data processing
  • Documentation and tutorials for reproducible research methods

These open contributions enable other researchers to validate findings, extend methodologies, and advance the field of computational biomechanics.

Research Interests

Digital Twins for Orthopaedics

Development of computational models that simulate musculoskeletal behavior to aid in diagnosis and treatment planning.

Finite Element Modeling

Creating detailed computational models of bones, joints, and soft tissues for biomechanical analysis.

In Vivo Data Capture

Using high-speed stereo radiography to capture real-world joint kinematics and validate computational models.

Computational Biomechanics

Modeling and simulation of musculoskeletal systems to understand joint mechanics and pathology.

Subject-Specific Modeling

Developing personalized computational models based on individual patient anatomy and physiology.

Open Science and Reproducibility

Providing research tools, data, and software to the scientific community to advance research.
